# Features Benefits Advantages (FBA)

**Status: DeskWolf core sales philosophy.** All marketing copy must appeal to **advantages first**, then touch benefits lightly, then anchor on one feature. Not optional. The skill's `copywriting_guidelines.md` references this file as Layer 0 (the lens that sits above voice, structure, anti-slop, and quality).

## The three layers

| Layer | What it is | What the customer hears |
|---|---|---|
| **Feature** | The spec. What it does on paper. | "It has an A14 chip and 6-core CPU." |
| **Benefit** | The experience of using it. How it feels. | "Apps open fast. The camera is sharp. It's smooth." |
| **ADVANTAGE** | The customer's **status shift** relative to other people. How they're *perceived*. | "My text bubbles are blue. My friends don't make fun of me. People think I'm successful." |

## The iPhone reasoning (canonical example)

Why does anyone actually want an iPhone? Not because of the specs. An Android matches or beats them on raw features. Not because of the experience either — a military phone has stronger benefits (indestructible, sealed, 5-day battery).

The actual purchase driver is the **advantage**: your text messages are blue, your friends don't make fun of you, the dating app match thinks you have money, your boss reads you as "the modern hire." Status is the real product.

**Most salespeople get stuck on features** — "sell me a pen" gets a spec list, and they lose the room.

**Okay marketers get to benefits** — they paint the experience of using the thing. Better. Most stop here.

**The top 1% lead with the advantage** — the status shift. They name what the customer's peers, customers, competitors, and family will think when they say yes.

## How to write with advantage in mind

Lead with the status shift, not the spec.

Every email body, every landing-page hero, every ad headline should answer:

> "How will my customers, my competitors, my peers, and my family see me differently if I say yes?"

Touch the benefit lightly. Anchor the feature as one specific detail (the "under 1 second," the "70+ languages," the "free design") because specifics make the status claim believable. Then close with a soft action.

### Three versions of the same line

**Feature-led (weak):**
> "Our AI voice agent answers calls in under a second and speaks 70+ languages."

**Benefit-led (okay):**
> "Stop losing after-hours calls. Sleep through the night knowing every lead is captured."

**Advantage-led (strong):**
> "When a customer calls your shop at 11pm and gets a real-feeling answer in Spanish, they tell their friends you've got it together. The franchise dental group on the next block can't say the same. Angel answers in under a second. Reply 'send the demo' and I'll show you how she sounds."

Notice the move: customer perception first, peer comparison second, feature last. That's the spine.

## When in doubt

Before sending any DeskWolf marketing draft, run two diagnostic questions:

1. **Status check:** Would the reader feel important if their best customer overheard them saying yes to this?
2. **Peer-comparison check:** Does this make them look ahead of, or behind, the people they care about being ahead of?

If the answer is no on either, the copy is still in feature/benefit territory. Rewrite.

## Hard rule for DeskWolf

> Marketing copy at DeskWolf always appeals to **advantages first**, then features. Benefits live in the middle as the connective tissue, lightly touched.

## TL;DR for the wall

> Features are specs. Benefits are how it feels.
> **Advantages are how they get seen.**
> Lead with how they get seen.
