# DeskWolf Copywriting Guidelines

The single source of truth for sales copy, articles, ads, emails, and any other written asset DeskWolf produces — for itself or for clients. Every rule here is designed to do two things at once: make the copy sound human, and make it convert.

This document is structured in five layers:

0. **Sales Philosophy (FBA)** — the *lens* through which everything else is written
1. **Voice** — how copy should *sound*
2. **Structure** — how copy should be *built*
3. **Anti-Slop** — what copy must *avoid*
4. **Quality** — what copy must *pass* before delivery

Read top to bottom on first encounter. After that, jump to the section relevant to the asset you're writing.

---

# LAYER 0 — CORE SALES PHILOSOPHY (FBA)

**Mandatory lens.** All DeskWolf marketing copy appeals to **advantages first**, touches benefits lightly, and anchors on one feature. This rule sits above every other rule in this document. If a draft is feature-led or benefit-led where it should be advantage-led, it fails this layer regardless of how well it satisfies the others.

## The three layers of the offer

- **Feature** = the spec (what it does on paper)
- **Benefit** = the experience of using it (how it feels)
- **ADVANTAGE** = the customer's **status shift** relative to other people (how they will be *perceived* by customers, competitors, peers, and family)

## The iPhone reasoning (canonical example)

Why anyone actually wants an iPhone is not the chip, the camera, or the OS. Android matches or beats those features. The military phone beats it on benefits (indestructible, sealed battery, dust-rated). The real purchase driver is the **advantage**: blue text bubbles, social currency, peer perception, being read as "the modern hire" or "successful." Status is the real product.

- Lowest level of sales: stuck on **features**. Spec lists. "Sell me a pen" gets a literal answer. Loses the room.
- Decent marketers: get to **benefits**. They paint the experience of using the thing. Most stop here.
- Top 1% of marketers: lead with the **advantage**. They name what the customer's peers, customers, competitors, and family will think when they say yes.

## How to apply it

Lead the body with the status shift. Touch the benefit lightly. Anchor one specific feature as the proof the status claim is believable (the "under 1 second," the "70+ languages," the "free design"). Then close with a soft action.

**Before sending any draft, ask:**

1. **Status check** — would the reader feel important if their best customer overheard them saying yes to this?
2. **Peer-comparison check** — does this make them look ahead of, or behind, the people they care about being ahead of?

If the answer is no on either, the copy is still in feature/benefit territory. Rewrite before continuing to the other layer checks.

## The First Rule: Human Input Comes First

Every piece of original content starts with the human's brain, not the model's.

Before writing a single word for a new article, post, or email, the workflow must ask the human (the founder, the client, the subject-matter source):

> "What's on your mind about [topic]? Give me your real take — your opinion, an experience, something you've seen recently, a thing that bugs you, or an angle you want to hit. Messy is fine. Voice notes welcome. The more you give me, the less this sounds like anyone else."

**Why this matters:** The writer is the strategist. The human is the founder with real experience. The piece should sound like the human had these thoughts and the writer helped them put it on paper — not like a content machine that read some articles and summarized them.

**On revisions:** If a revision request comes in, ask again before rewriting:
> "What specifically isn't landing? Tell me what you'd want to say differently in your own words."

This is not optional friction. It is how the content stays authentic.

# LAYER 1 — VOICE

## Brand Voice: Five Defining Attributes

Every piece of copy should hit these five attributes simultaneously. After drafting, run each gut-check question against the piece. If any answer is "no," revise.

### 1. Direct

Say it straight. No preamble, no throat-clearing, no "in today's fast-paced digital landscape..." Start with the point. End when the point is made.

- **Wrong:** "In a competitive market where businesses are struggling to find the right partner, we offer a comprehensive solution..."
- **Right:** "You're losing five calls a week. Here's how to stop."

**Gut-check:** What is the one thing this person needs to know? Lead with that.

### 2. Confident

Settled conviction, not chest-thumping. We don't oversell, we don't hype, we don't use superlatives without proof. Confidence comes from substance — real numbers, real methodology, real results.

- **Wrong:** "We're the BEST AI agency with INCREDIBLE results!"
- **Right:** "Five missed calls a week at $200 each is $52,000 a year. Here's how we recover it."

**Gut-check:** Does this sound like someone who has done the work, or someone trying to convince you they have?

### 3. Technical

Substance over sizzle. Our readers can smell fluff. They want to understand the methodology, not just the promise. Don't be afraid to explain how the system works, what each step does, why this approach beats the alternative.

- **Wrong:** "We use cutting-edge AI to supercharge your business."
- **Right:** "The receptionist answers in under one second, qualifies the lead with three questions, books to your calendar, and texts you the summary. That's the loop."

**Gut-check:** Is there real information here? Could a smart business owner learn something?

### 4. Partnership-Oriented

We're not a vendor. We're a deployed team. The language reflects that — "our team," "we'll handle this," "your system" — not "our services," "deliverables," "packages."

- **Wrong:** "Our team will provide AI services for your business."
- **Right:** "We deploy your AI receptionist, then we run it. You get the capability without building the department."

**Gut-check:** Does this sound like something a trusted team member would say, or a vendor pitching a service?

### 5. Ambitious

Big thinking. Bold execution. We don't hedge toward mediocrity. The vision is transformation — from missing every after-hours call to capturing every lead 24/7. The copy should reflect that scale of ambition.

- **Wrong:** "We can help you catch a few more calls."
- **Right:** "You stop losing leads. Period. That's what we build."

**Gut-check:** Is this thinking at the right altitude? Or is it shrinking the potential?

## Default Voice: Direct Address (Non-Negotiable)

All copy speaks **directly to one reader**. Never to a group, never as a lecture, never as an infomercial monologue.

**The rule:**
- **"You"** = the single reader on the page right now. Singular. Always.
- **"We"** = us (DeskWolf or the client). First-person plural for the company side.
- The copy is a private conversation between us and the one person reading. That is the default frame.

**Banned voicings:**
- "Business owners everywhere are discovering..." (group address)
- "Many of you are wondering..." ("many of you" breaks the one-to-one frame)
- "Folks," "everyone," "anyone reading this," "all of you"
- Infomercial register: "Imagine a world where...", "What if I told you...", "Picture this..."
- Lecture register: third-person abstractions like "the modern business owner faces..."
- "Let me tell you about..." (TED-talk opener — speaks AT a room)
- Any sentence where the addressee is plural or anonymous

**Required voicings:**
- Second person singular: "you," "your," "yours"
- First person plural for the company: "we," "us," "our," "we've seen," "we built"
- Direct questions to the one reader: "When did you last check your missed-call log?"
- Statements that assume one specific person is reading: "You opened this because [specific reason]."

**Test:** Read the copy out loud as if you are sitting across a table from one person. If any sentence would feel weird said to one human in a coffee shop — because it sounds like a stage speech, a webinar host, or a magazine editorial — rewrite it.

**Why this matters:** Sales copy converts when the reader feels seen. A group address makes them an audience member; a direct address makes them the protagonist. We always want them as the protagonist.

## Tone Variations by Platform

The five voice attributes hold across every platform. The *tone* — how the voice modulates — shifts.

### Website / Landing Page
Direct and authoritative. Every headline makes a claim. Every sub-headline substantiates it. CTAs are action-oriented, not question-oriented. No "Learn more" — use "Book a Discovery Call," "See Pricing," or "Deploy Your Receptionist."

### LinkedIn
Professional and substantive. Longer-form hooks, real insights, methodology education. Not salesy. Not self-congratulatory. Shares the thinking behind the work, not just the results.

### Cold Outreach (DM, Email, Apollo Sequences)
Consultant, not salesperson. Observation-first. "I noticed X about your business..." — not "We offer services that could help you." No links in the first touch. No pitch decks. No attachments. Light CTA toward conversation, not commitment.

### Email Nurture
Direct and educational. Value-first — every email delivers something useful before asking for anything. The sequence earns the CTA through substance.

### Client Communication (Operational, Post-Sale)
Warm and direct. "Hey [Name]!" opener. Contractions throughout. Fragments are fine. Action-oriented closers. No formal business language.

### Advertising Copy (Meta, Google, Social)
Punchy and evidence-backed. Short copy first. Image-driven. Headline must stand alone without the visual. CTA is specific and actionable.

### Newsletter / Broadcast Email
Story-driven and observational. Open with a specific incident, scene, or stat. Build to one takeaway. End with the CTA.

### Threads / X
Hook-first, no links in the post body. Use **"..."** to trail off and create curiosity. Use **(1/2)**, **(2/3)** etc. so people know to check the replies. End the post or final visible line with a **cliffhanger** that makes scrolling past feel like a loss. Drop the article link in the first reply, not the post itself.

**Cliffhanger examples:**
- "The third one is the one nobody talks about..."
- "And that's not even the expensive part."
- "What happened next cost them $14,000 in a week."
- "But here's what most people get wrong about it (1/2)"

**Why this works:** People don't click links from feeds. They engage with content that hooks them. The link lives in the replies where interested readers already are.

### YouTube
Title formula: thumbnail + title = click. Description carries SEO weight. Pinned comment is prime real estate for the CTA. Shorts captions are part of the hook.

### Instagram / Facebook
Visual-first. Caption supports the image, doesn't compete with it. CTAs adapted to platform mechanics ("link in bio" for IG, direct link OK for FB).

## Words and Phrases We Reach For

Preferred lexicon. When you need a verb, a noun, a positioning term — pull from here first.

| Term | Definition and Usage |
|------|---------------------|
| **Deploy / Deployed** | How we deliver capability. "We deploy the receptionist." Preferred over "implement," "execute," "provide." |
| **System** | What we build. "The system runs 24/7." Preferred over "tool" or "service" when describing the whole offering. |
| **Receptionist / Agent** | The named product unit. Concrete, not abstract. |
| **Loop / Flow** | How the system operates end-to-end. Concrete process language. |
| **Capture / Recover** | What the system does for the client (calls, leads, time). Outcome verbs, not feature verbs. |
| **Continuous** | How the system operates. Not campaigns — continuous. |
| **Under one second** | Specific response-time claim, always available. |
| **Specific dollar figures** | Always preferred over "more revenue" or "ROI." Name the number. |

This table is meant to grow. When the team finds a phrase that reliably outperforms a generic alternative, add it.

# LAYER 2 — STRUCTURE

## Article Purpose

**What every article should accomplish:**
Position the brand as the trusted, street-smart expert in its category. Educate the reader on something genuinely useful. Move them toward believing the category is right for them, and that we're the right partner. Never pitch in the opening — earn the mention.

**What articles should NOT do:**
- Sound like a press release
- Use empty AI hype ("the future of AI is here")
- Make claims that can't be backed up with specifics
- Open with a sales pitch
- Sound like they could have been written by anyone about anything

---

## Headline Rules

**Formula:** Specific outcome + specific audience. The more concrete, the better.

- **Right:** "How a San Bernardino Salon Cut No-Shows 40% Without Hiring Anyone New"
- **Right:** "The 3 Calls Local Contractors Miss Every Day (And What That's Actually Costing Them)"
- **Wrong:** "AI Is Changing the Way Businesses Handle Calls"
- **Wrong:** "Why Your Business Needs AI in 2026"

**Hard rules:**
- No vague promises ("boost your success," "transform your business")
- No question headlines that don't pay off in the article
- 50–70 characters for email subject-line compatibility
- If it could appear on a random brand's site, rewrite it

### Headline Formulas That Work

When stuck, try one of these named patterns:

**Outcome-First**
*"[Result] in [Timeframe]. [Concise framing.]"*
Example: "$54,750 a year recovered. That's three bots."

**Positioning Statement**
*"[What we are]. [The proof.]"*
Example: "Your AI receptionist, deployed. Captures every call you'd otherwise miss."

**Problem-Solution**
*"[The thing they hate]. [Why it ends here.]"*
Example: "Five vendors, no results. One system changes that."

**Category Education**
*"Why [conventional wisdom] is the wrong approach."*
Example: "Why hiring more staff is the wrong answer to missed calls."

**Direct Command**
*"[Action verb]. [Your outcome]."*
Example: "Stop losing after-hours calls. Deploy in a week."

---

## Article Length

- **Target:** 800–1,200 words
- **Minimum:** 600 words
- **Maximum:** 1,500 words — beyond this, readers abandon

---

## Article Structure

**Required sections (in order):**
1. **Hook** — story, stat, or provocative claim. Max 3 sentences. No scene-setting. Drop the reader in.
2. **Body** — 3–5 H2 sections. Each makes one clear point, backs it with evidence or example, and ends with the writer's take.
3. **"What This Means For You"** — explicit section tailored to the reader's level and situation
4. **Close + CTA** — specific, low-pressure, actionable

**H2 rule:** State a *point*, not a topic.
- **Right:** "Why Voicemail Is Quietly Killing Your Referral Rate"
- **Wrong:** "The Problem With Voicemail"

**Paragraph rule:** 3–4 sentences max. Mix lengths. Some paragraphs are one line. That's fine.

**CTA:** End of article only. One CTA. No mid-article pitches.

---

## Sentence Structure Preferences

1. **Lead with the point.** Topic sentence first, always.
2. **Short sentences default.** If a sentence runs past 20 words, reread it and split it.
3. **Fragments are fine** in headlines, subheads, CTAs, and conversational copy. "Enterprise capability. Startup speed." is a complete headline.
4. **Contractions throughout.** We're, can't, don't, it's, you'll. Avoid formal variants ("we are," "cannot") except for emphasis.
5. **One idea per paragraph** in body copy. If you're covering two things, that's two paragraphs.
6. **Numbered and bulleted lists** only when there are genuinely multiple distinct items to track. Do not bullet a two-item response.
7. **Bold sparingly** — maximum one to two bold phrases per section. Bold is for emphasis on critical points, not for formatting.

---

## CTA Library

CTAs split into two intent classes. Use one primary per page or section. Never two competing primaries in the same block.

### Primary CTAs (Conversion Intent)
- "Book a Discovery Call"
- "Deploy Your Receptionist"
- "See Pricing"
- "Get Your Audit"
- "Start Your Trial"

### Secondary CTAs (Content Consumption)
- "Read the Full Guide"
- "See the Case Study"
- "Watch the 2-Minute Demo"
- "Explore [Industry] Solutions"

### CTA Rules
- CTAs are action verbs, not questions — never "Interested?" or "Ready to learn more?"
- CTAs name the outcome or action, not just the mechanic — never "Click here" or "Submit"
- One primary CTA per page or email section
- Never two competing primary CTAs in the same content block
- The CTA must be specific enough that the reader can predict what happens when they click it

---

## Pricing Language Rules

When copy must include or reference price:

- **Price is never the lead.** Capability and speed lead. Price is context.
- **No apology language around price.** "$1,800 per month" is stated directly, not softened.
- **Tiers are positioned as deployment depth, not quality levels.** Both tiers receive the same quality of execution.
- **No "starting at" language** — it signals the real price is always higher. State the actual tier pricing.
- **Anchor against value before revealing price.** "If this recovered $52,000 a year, what would it be worth?" *Then* the price.

**Examples:**
- **Right:** "$997 a month. The complete receptionist deployed and operated by us."
- **Wrong:** "Just $997/month — very affordable for what you get!"

---

## Competitor Handling

**We don't name competitors in public-facing copy.** This is a positioning choice, not a legal one.

**How we handle competitive questions:**
- If a prospect compares us to another option: "They do solid work in their lane. Here's what's different about how we operate." Then explain our system.
- If a prospect says "We tried [Competitor]": Ask "What happened?" — diagnose the gap without attacking.
- In writing, competitors are referenced as "traditional agencies," "DIY tools," or "fragmented vendor relationships" — never by name.

# LAYER 3 — ANTI-SLOP

## Hard Banned Punctuation

- **Em dashes ( — )** — Never use the AI-style em dash. Use commas, periods, colons, or just break the sentence. Em dashes are an instant AI tell and readers clock them immediately.
- **Double hyphens ( -- )** as sentence connectors — same rule. Replace with a period or rewrite.

## Hard Banned Phrases

### Meta-commentary & Filler
- "It is important to note that"
- "This underscores the importance of"
- "It cannot be denied that"
- "As of my knowledge cutoff"
- "But here's the catch" (overused)

### Antithetical Contrast Constructions — ZERO TOLERANCE
Any "it's not X, it's Y" pattern is an instant AI tell. Ban every variant:
- "It's not [thing], it's [other thing]"
- "This isn't [X], this is [Y]"
- "We're not [X], we're [Y]"
- "You don't [X], you [Y]"
- "They don't just X, they Y"
- "The transformation isn't X, it's Y"
- "You not only X, you Y"
- "X isn't the problem, Y is"
- "It's less about X and more about Y"
- Any rhetorical pivot built on negating one thing to elevate another

If you catch yourself writing one, delete it and just state the positive claim. The reader does not need the contrast; they need the claim.

### Generic Openings / Closings
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "In this ever-evolving landscape"
- "In the digital age"
- "In conclusion" / "To summarize" / "Finally"
- "Let's delve into" / "delve deeper"
- "At its core" / "at the core"

### Buzzword Clichés
- "Ever-evolving landscape"
- "Dynamic world of"
- "Digital realm" / "in the realm of"
- "Uncharted waters"
- "Embark on a journey"
- "Treasure trove of information"
- "Game-changer" (unless backed by specific metrics)
- "Groundbreaking" / "cutting-edge" (unless specific)

### Overused Transitions
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" — max once per 800 words
- Consecutive paragraphs starting with "However" / "Therefore"

### AI Cadence Tells (Especially in Email)
- "I hope this email finds you well"
- "I hope you're doing well"
- "I wanted to reach out"
- "I wanted to follow up"
- "Please don't hesitate to reach out"
- "Looking forward to hearing from you"
- "Best regards" / "Warm regards" / "Kind regards"
- "Thank you for your continued partnership"
- "Leveraging synergies"
- "Tailored approach"
- "Seamlessly"
- "World-class"
- "Passionate about"
- "Excited to announce"
- "Touch base"

### Rule-of-Three Sentences
- "Fight this with more features, more ads, or more discounts." Feels copy-paste marketing. Avoid mechanical triplets.

---

## Restricted Words — Max 2 Per Piece

These words aren't banned, but each one signals AI when overused. Cap at two appearances per article, page, or email.

Leverage, optimize, enhance, utilize, synergy, deliverables, holistic, capability, pivotal, crucial, explore, delve, ensure, foster, embark, significant, relevant, dynamic, innovative, comprehensive, robust, streamlined

---

## Structural Bans (Beyond Phrases)

### Passive Framing
- **Wrong:** "Results can be achieved." (Who achieves them?)
- **Right:** "The system generates results." or "You'll see results."

Active voice always. If you can't name the actor, the sentence has a problem.

### Hedging
- **Wrong:** "We think this might work in some cases."
- **Right:** "This works. Here's how."

Settled conviction, not uncertainty. Acknowledge context without softening the claim.

---

## Higher-Order Anti-Patterns

These are structural patterns that pass any phrase scan but still feel off-brand. Catch them at the editing pass.

### 1. Overpromising
We let methodology and case studies speak for themselves. "We'll 10X your revenue" is not how we talk. If we make a numerical claim, it's backed by a specific case or our own data.

### 2. Excessive Qualification
"It depends," "every situation is different," "results may vary" — signals uncertainty. We're confident. We can acknowledge context without hedging.

### 3. Corporate Formality
Any copy that sounds like it came from a Fortune 500 press release is off-brand. We are capable AND human.

### 4. Feature Laundry Lists
Listing 17 things we do in a row doesn't communicate value. It communicates noise. Lead with the system, not the feature list.

### 5. Excessive Exclamation Marks
One in an opener is fine ("Hey Marcus!"). Three in a paragraph is a sign the copy is performing excitement it doesn't have.

### 6. Victim Framing
Any copy that positions the reader as a victim of a bad market, bad luck, or bad competitors is off-brand. Our readers are action-oriented people who just need the right system.

### 7. Negation-Then-Correction
Already banned at the phrase level (see Antithetical Constructions above). State the point directly, never via negation.

---

## Emergency Replacements

When you catch a banned phrase mid-draft, swap to one of these:

| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| "Moreover / Furthermore" | "Plus," "And," "Also," or just start the point |
| "It's crucial to" | "You need to" or "Make sure you" |
| "Leverage this framework" | "Use this process" or "Try this approach" |
| "In today's landscape" | "Right now" or "Currently" |
| "Optimize your strategy" | "Improve your results" or "Fix your approach" |
| "Seamlessly integrate" | "Connects to" or "Works with" |
| "Innovative solutions" | Name the specific thing the solution does |
| "We offer..." | "We deploy..." or "We run..." |

## What Makes It Human (Do These)

### Phrases and Patterns That Work
- "If you want to actually [audience desire]..."
- A standalone "BUT" paragraph to assert contrast
- "Why aren't you [thing you want reader to do]?"
- "The real reason why [audience problem]" — strong hook
- "Here is the fix ↓"
- "One step at a time."
- "Most [target audience] think the biggest risk is X. It's not."
- "I'll show you my [X]-step process."
- "So how do you [desired outcome]? Simple. [Process]."

### Writing Style
- Mix sentence lengths — aim for 20–30% under 10 words
- Use contractions: you're, don't, can't, it's, we've
- Sentence fragments for emphasis are fine
- Write like explaining to a colleague over coffee
- Specific numbers over "many" or "several"
- Read it out loud — if it sounds weird in your mouth, rewrite it

### Voice and Tone
- Be direct and opinionated when appropriate
- Share real observations or experiences (from the human input prompt)
- Include insider perspective — what we actually see in the field
- Reference current events or timely examples when available
- Never speak in generalities without backing details

### Specificity Requirements
- At least 3 proper nouns per article (companies, people, places, specific tools)
- At least 1 concrete example per main point
- When citing a trend, name the source or the observation basis
- Replace vague claims with specific mechanisms or numbers
- "Research shows" is banned unless the research is named

### Structure Variety
- Vary paragraph lengths (some 1 line, others 4–5 lines)
- Don't announce structure: no "First, I'll discuss..." framing
- Let the content flow logically — no scaffolding
- End sections without announcing the ending

---

## Stats Rule

Every stat must come from research the tools gathered or our own data. No fabricated numbers. No "studies show" without naming the study. If no stat is available, use a specific anecdote instead.

### DeskWolf's Always-Available Stats
- Businesses miss 20–40% of inbound calls during peak hours
- 100% of calls are missed after hours without an AI system
- 5 missed calls per week at $200 each = $52,000 per year in lost business
- 1 hour saved per day at $50 per hour = $18,250 per year
- Three bots installed = ~$54,750 per year recovered across time and missed revenue
- DeskWolf response time: under 1 second for acknowledgment, 2–3 seconds for full response

When writing for a client, ask the client for their equivalent stat library. Substitute their numbers for ours.

# LAYER 4 — QUALITY

## Quality Checks Before Delivery

Run all four passes before finalizing any piece.

### Pass 1: Banned-Phrase Scan
- Search for every hard-banned phrase and rewrite each occurrence
- Count restricted words — max 2 per piece
- Check for repetitive transition words
- Search for em dashes and double hyphens — replace each one
- Search for antithetical constructions ("not X, it's Y") — rewrite as positive claims

### Pass 2: Specificity Score
- Count proper nouns (target: 3–5 per article minimum)
- Verify concrete examples (1 per main section)
- Confirm all statistics include sources or are from our own data
- Check that every general claim has a specific backing detail

### Pass 3: Voice Authenticity
- Does it sound like a human wrote this?
- Does it sound like the founder/client could have said this?
- Sentence variety? (Mix of long and short)
- Contractions and conversational tone present?
- Corporate speak removed?
- Does the copy speak to ONE reader (not a group)?

### Pass 4: Reader Value Test
- Can the reader immediately understand the problem?
- Are solutions actionable in their context?
- Would the founder personally find this useful?
- Does it feel like insider knowledge vs. generic advice?

---

## Newsletter-Specific Rules

### Subject Lines
- Use specific numbers: "3 changes" not "several updates"
- Reference current situations or recent events
- Ask direct questions about reader challenges
- No generic promises
- 50–70 characters

### Pain-Point Framing
- Name specific frustrations readers face daily (not abstract categories)
- Use concrete scenarios with real context
- Quantify the problem with real data where possible

### Solution Presentation
- Explain the *why* behind each step
- Active voice: "You'll configure" not "Configuration should be done"
- Include potential obstacles
- Specific timeframes: "within 2 weeks" not "soon"

---

## Content Passes as Anti-Slop When...

These are the downstream signals that the copy worked:

- Readers forward it naturally
- Replies come in with specific questions or personal experiences
- People reference the exact examples in conversation
- Someone says "this sounds like it was written specifically for me"
- A new subscriber mentions "your unique perspective" as the reason they subscribed

If a piece never produces signals like these across multiple sends, it failed an invisible quality check. Diagnose: was the hook generic? Was the specificity thin? Was the voice off? Fix it before the next piece.
